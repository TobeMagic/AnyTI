## What Made SBTI Explode

### 1. It names states, not personalities

Multiple analyses converge on the same point: SBTI does not really win by sounding more accurate. It wins because it translates messy daily emotional states into short labels that feel like “人话” instead of psychometric vocabulary. The useful insight is not “copy MBTI harder”; it is “compress a blurry feeling into one label users instantly recognize and want to repeat.”

**Implication for AnyTI:** archetypes should behave like social labels first, explanatory types second.

### 2. The result is short, sharp, and screenshot-native

The result card is not gentle or formal. It is short, a little mean, a little funny, and highly legible in screenshot form. That matters because the sharing unit is not an essay; it is a card someone can post in a feed, group chat, or comment thread.

**Implication for AnyTI:** result output needs a hierarchy of `label -> one-line hit -> short explanation -> optional deeper detail`, not long academic copy blocks.

### 3. The completion cost is tiny

Public writeups emphasize a low barrier: around 31 questions, only a few minutes, no setup burden, no heavy explanatory tax. This made “just try it” socially cheap.

**Implication for AnyTI:** first-run tests should stay short enough to finish impulsively. Friction kills virality faster than imperfect science.

### 4. It is built for list behavior and social comparison

Even mirrors of the product foreground `Type Gallery`, `Leaderboard`, and featured profiles. That means the product is not only “take a test,” but also “browse types,” “compare outcomes,” and “see what others got.” This is where the user's requirement for “传播列表属性” becomes concrete.

**Implication for AnyTI:** every category should support list surfaces, not just a single result page. Examples:
- category home page with featured archetypes
- future type gallery page
- recommendation cards that feel collectible, not generic

### 5. It spreads because it behaves like content first

One strong analysis argues that SBTI worked as a content probe more than a durable product. The “first hit” mattered more than product polish. That does not mean stability is irrelevant; it means the growth edge came from the first social strike, not backend sophistication.

**Implication for AnyTI:** do not overengineer retention or business systems before the first strong social-expression loop is proven.

### 6. Its creator framed it as entertainment, not authority

Public reporting repeatedly notes that the creator said it was for fun, not professional psychology and not a serious commercial instrument. That lowered resistance and helped the product feel playful instead of fake-authoritative.

**Implication for AnyTI:** keep disclaimers lightweight and explicit. “Inspired by” beats “scientifically proves.”

## How AnyTI Should Beat It

### Keep these advantages

- Human-language labels that instantly feel recognizable
- Short test duration and low start cost
- Screenshot-ready result cards
- Hidden/easter-egg archetypes
- Social comparison surfaces

### Improve where SBTI is weak

- Stronger reusable engine and cleaner content-pack architecture
- Stable homepage -> category page -> test page information architecture
- Category-specific theme systems that make the matrix feel like a designed product, not a one-off meme page
- More durable route and SEO strategy
- Cleaner bridge from result page to next test
- Better poster quality and more reliable export behavior

### Product Moves for AnyTI

1. **Design archetypes as three-layer objects**
   - Screenshot label: short, funny, instantly relayable
   - One-line hit: the sentence users quote in chats
   - Short rationale: enough explanation to feel “不是乱说”

2. **Make category pages into list surfaces**
   - Each category page should preview notable archetypes, not just one CTA button
   - This supports the user's request for “传播列表属性”

3. **Use category color as memory glue**
   - Love = rose family
   - Work = colder steel/slate tones
   - Study = bright yellow/ink
   - Travel = airy teal/sky
   - The color helps users remember the lane even before they remember the acronym

4. **Prioritize “can I share this?” before “is this complete?”**
   - If a result cannot work as a poster, a card, and a feed screenshot, the core loop is unfinished

5. **Avoid copying SBTI’s rough edges**
   - Do not imitate instability, throwaway routing, or purely improvisational content structure
   - Beat it with better system design while keeping the same emotional sharpness

## AnyTI Content Rules Derived from This Research

- Use category-specific internet language, not generic psych language
- Write labels that read cleanly in poster cards and recommendation lists
- Prefer “state” and “vibe” naming over diagnostic naming
- Let humor cut first; let explanation follow
- Keep the tone playful, not clinical
